About

Hi! I'm Baron. I'm an aspiring artist that works with traditional art and digital art, who also dabbles in programming sometimes. I made this site as a way to practice web development, and also to showcase my various work in art, design, and development.

Right now, my focus is on making assets for indie game projects, including portraits, UI, design, music, scripts, and animations. In the future, I want to use my experiences working on my personal projects and collaborating with a team on community projects to be a graphic designer who works with interactive media and design.

Please, take a look!

Digital Art

My digital portfolio mainly consists of pixel art portraits and animations drawn with a tablet and mouse. There are also process gifs showcasing the assets before insertion into the game.

A collection of 63 portraits of original character designs and characters from other media. These were drawn over 3 years. For these portraits, I had to think outside the box due to the limited 96x80 box size and the limit of a 15-color indexed palette. Each portrait also has animated eye-blink/mouth-speaking frames as well.

A few gifs showcasing my process for digital pixel-art portraits. I usually either block-out shapes or start with line-art, before shading and then anti-aliasing.

A compilation of SRPG combat animations I made for indie games I developed using existing engines. In order: Attack, Critical Hit, Dodge Attack, and if applicable, Sword/Staff Spell Cast.

Each animation loops back to the indie pose, have a dynamic palette for quicker repalettes, and are scripted with frame-timings, the game engine's sound effects, and code. The total frames per animation range from 3 to 86. Critical Hit animations have the most frames and are usually in the 70ish frames range.

The 'Spell Loop' of each animation is the looping 'dramatic cape flap' animation played when the unit casts a spell, until the end of the spell's animation.

An example of the animation scripting in-engine; there are frame-timings, sound effects, and code to determine the behavior of the current frame.

Projects

I have had the opportunity to work on several indie FE fangame projects built off existing engines, both on my own and with a team. These experiences have been extremely valuable for me, as I have been able to learn about art creation and programming simultaneously, while also collaborating with my peers.

I'm the project and art lead of an indie FE fangame project, Project Eternal Winter. For this project, I have made portraits, animations, map animations, UI, icons, and non-visual art elements, such as writing, development, and music. In addition, I've also worked on asset creation and programming for several different community fangames, including Call of the Armor and Bells of Byelen.

Project Eternal Winter was a chance for me to learn and practice asset creation and game development on my own, while also dealing with hardware constraints such as pixel size, portrait box size, and restricted palette order. I was able to challenge myself by implementing these assets into the game while working on my time management skills.

The next projects I worked on were collaborative 'telephone' projects, named after the childhood game of telephone. We kept track of changes through a spreadsheet, but otherwise passed the game files around to be modified with minimal communication between chapters. This resulted in very varied art, music, writing, and gameplay design. These projects were incredibly valuable for me, as I learned how to manage a team, balance my workload with my education, and learn alongside many different people from all over the world. The goal was to create a group project that maintained a level of consistency and quality similar to smaller group or solo developer projects, while also taking advantage of the speed that group efforts often offer. These were really fun to be work on, and I'm grateful for the opportunity to participate in and learn from these projects.

Call of the Armor was the first 'telephone' project I worked on with a team. There were 14 people on the team, and this project introduced me to creating portraits, writing, and animation.

Bells of Byelen was the second project I worked on with a team. The team consisted of 15 people, with most members being from CotA, including myself. This project allowed me to further develop my skills in portrait creation, as well as experiment with creating animations and UI elements.
